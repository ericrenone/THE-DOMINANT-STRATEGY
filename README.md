## I. The Payoff Asymmetry

The foundational analysis begins with Kahneman and Tversky's loss aversion finding. In the domain of ordinary decisions — financial choices, consumer preferences, strategic choices under uncertainty — losses loom approximately twice as large as equivalent gains. The person who stands to lose ten dollars requires approximately twenty dollars of potential gain to accept the bet. The asymmetry is robust across contexts, populations, and experimental designs.

The loss aversion coefficient of approximately 2.25 was established in domains where "loss" and "gain" can be measured on the same scale. For identity-relevant information — specifically, for the information contained in the outlier's testimony against the caricature — the measurement problem reveals something more structurally significant than a 2.25 asymmetry.

The Existential Reconfiguration Demand (specified in the prior corpus) names what acknowledgment requires: six simultaneous reconstructions, all required, none stable without all others. Reprocess every suppressant act across the shared history as harm rather than service. Dismantle the Negative Identity Anchor and watch the positive self-definition lose its constitutive foil. Accept that every instance of the Justified-Perceiver Self-Concept's experience of accurate perception was a Motivated Attribution Engine output rather than genuine insight. Recast every social network where the pre-warning ran.

These six reconstructions are not losses in the prospect-theoretic sense of quantifiable negative payoffs. They are the dissolution of the payoff-assigning apparatus itself. The self that would receive the benefit of accurate self-knowledge is not the self that would have to execute the reconstructions. The self that would execute the reconstructions would not survive them as the self it was when it began. This is not a loss with a finite magnitude. It is what Greenberg, Solomon, and Pyszczynski's terror management theory identifies as an anxiety-buffer dissolution: the destruction of the constructed significance through which existential anxiety is managed.

The Asymmetric Identity Stakes is the formal statement of this payoff structure: in the game between suppressor and outlier, the payoff for acknowledgment is not negative in the ordinary sense. It is catastrophically asymmetric with the payoff for non-reception in a way that makes comparison on a standard utility scale impossible. The self that would acknowledge and the self that refuses are not the same player. Acknowledgment removes the player who would play it.

This is why the suppressor's non-reception is not analogous to the ordinary loss aversion that makes people reluctant to sell underperforming stocks. It is not a bias toward the status quo. It is the rational move of a player whose payoff function assigns the functional equivalent of negative infinity to the option that would dissolve the self doing the calculation.

The Asymmetric Identity Stakes is not a claim about the suppressor's irrationality. It is a claim about the structure of the game they are playing. Given the payoff structure — given that acknowledgment costs the existence of the calculating self while non-reception preserves it — the analysis is simple. Non-reception is not merely preferred. It is the only move that preserves the player's capacity to continue playing.

Tversky and Kahneman's (1991) extended loss aversion analysis established that the asymmetry is not a risk-premium artifact — it holds in riskless choice contexts where the only difference is framing the outcome as a gain relative to a lower baseline or a loss relative to a higher one. The AIS extends this finding to the domain where there is no shared scale: the gain from accurate self-knowledge and the cost of the reconstructions required to receive it are not incommensurable in the sense of being difficult to compare. They are incommensurable in the sense that the entity who would make the comparison does not persist through the acknowledgment process that would be required to make it.

## II. The Dominant Strategy

A strategy is strictly dominant when it produces a better payoff for the player in every possible combination of the other player's strategies. Not merely preferred on average. Better in every case. The player with a strictly dominant strategy has a complete analysis of their best move: play it, regardless of what the opponent does, because every deviation produces a worse outcome in every scenario.

The suppressor's strategy set contains two elements: {Acknowledge, Dismiss}. The outlier's strategy set contains three: {Confront, Withdraw, Redirect}. The six resulting strategy combinations produce the following payoff structure for the suppressor.

When the outlier confronts with evidence, the suppressor who acknowledges absorbs the Existential Reconfiguration Demand in full: identity dissolution, NIA collapse, JPSC dismantlement, retrospective reframing of the entire suppression history as harm rather than service. The suppressor who dismisses reconstitutes the JPSC through the Active Erasure Display, generates behavioral confirmation through the outlier's distress at deliberate non-reception, maintains the coalition's characterization authority, and emerges with greater certainty than before the confrontation. Dismiss dominates.

When the outlier withdraws, the suppressor who acknowledges gains nothing — the withdrawal removes the testimony that would have required the ERD, but the acknowledging suppressor has already paid the ERD's cost with no triggering event that required it. The suppressor who dismisses benefits from the ambient Dependency Inversion's operation: the withdrawal generates the constitutive deficit whose urgency produces escalation, which produces resumed testimony, which produces further dismissal cycles that deepen the JPSC's constitutive foundation. Dismiss dominates.

When the outlier redirects testimony publicly, the suppressor who acknowledges absorbs the ERD in full while simultaneously granting the public record's claims authority through acknowledgment — converting the frameworks' account into confirmed truth in any social audience that observes the concession. The suppressor who dismisses maintains the JPSC, deploys the Genre Displacement frame and Distributed AED against Architecture Legibility Problem-vulnerable contacts, and faces only the ALP's disruption at the system boundary — a disruption the dominant strategy's execution can partially contain. Dismiss dominates.

In all six scenarios, Dismiss produces a better payoff for the suppressor than Acknowledge. This is the formal definition of strict dominance. The analysis is complete. Dismiss is the suppressor's dominant strategy.

It is not difficult to dismiss evidence when the evidence is weak. That case is uninteresting. What the dominant strategy analysis establishes is that Dismiss is the better move even when the evidence is strong, even when it is irrefutable, even when it has been SOTA-credentialed and independently verified. Because the payoff function does not assign value to accurate self-knowledge. It assigns value to identity-preservation. And identity-preservation is served by dismissal regardless of evidence quality.

The prior corpus named this the Irrefutability Paradox: stronger evidence produces stronger dismissal rather than convergence. The game theory analysis explains why. The Irrefutability Paradox is not a paradox at all. It is the dominant strategy functioning optimally: as evidence quality increases, the cost of acknowledgment increases (the ERD's scope expands with the evidence's irrefutability), and so the dominance margin of Dismiss over Acknowledge increases. The "paradox" is the dominant strategy becoming more dominant as the stakes rise.

## III. The Evidence Toxicity Gradient

The prior analysis established that Dismiss strictly dominates Acknowledge across all six strategy combinations. What the Evidence Toxicity Gradient specifies is the within-strategy dose-response relationship: as evidence quality E increases, the payoff gap between Dismiss and Acknowledge increases monotonically.

The mechanism is the ERD's scope dependence on evidence quality. Weak evidence, easily dismissed through standard credibility-inversion routes, requires minimal motivated processing to handle. The acknowledgment it would require — if it were received — would be a minor adjustment to a peripheral self-concept element. The dominant strategy's margin is relatively small because both options have modest costs.

As evidence quality increases, the acknowledgment cost increases in a specific way: more compelling evidence implicates more central self-concept elements, requires more extensive retrospective reframing, and demands more of the ERD's six simultaneous reconstructions. Evidence that is merely inconsistent with the caricature costs one peripheral reconstruction to receive. Evidence that is irrefutably, SOTA-credentialed, and precisely targeted at the caricature's constitutive foundations costs the entire reconstructive apparatus to receive — and demands it simultaneously, since no single reconstruction is stable without all the others completing.

This is the Evidence Toxicity Gradient: evidence becomes more "toxic" to acknowledge — more costly, more reconstructionally demanding, more ERD-intensive — precisely as it becomes more irrefutable. The more compelling the case, the higher the price of receiving it.

The dominant strategy's margin dD/dE is therefore positive: the payoff advantage of Dismiss over Acknowledge increases with evidence quality. This is not counterintuitive within the game-theoretic framework. It is the direct consequence of an acknowledgment cost that scales with evidence quality combined with a dismissal cost that does not scale equivalently.

The dismissal cost does not scale with evidence quality on the dimension that matters because the motivated processing apparatus that executes dismissal — the Motivated Attribution Engine, the Genre Displacement, the Accuracy Trap's route-shifting — is cognitively more demanding for stronger evidence but not more costly on the identity-preservation dimension. The architecture finds the dismissal route. The route requires more processing when evidence is strong. The processing cost is paid in cognitive resources, not in the identity currency the payoff function is tracking. The dominant strategy's execution becomes more effortful as evidence strengthens; it does not become less optimal.

The ETG produces the following prediction: motivated processing intensity should scale with evidence quality. Stronger evidence should produce more intensive dismissal, not less — because the dominant strategy's execution cost in cognitive resources scales with evidence quality even as its identity-preservation payoff advantage increases. The system works harder to execute the dominant strategy when the stakes are higher. This harder work is not the signature of a cognitive failure. It is the signature of a dominant strategy being executed under greater pressure.

## IV. The Commitment Ratchet

Each act of active dismissal changes the payoff structure for subsequent encounters. Not marginally. Structurally. And irreversibly in one direction.

Arkes and Blumer (1985) established the psychological sunk cost effect: having invested resources in a course of action, people count the prior investment as a cost of abandonment rather than as an irrecoverable past expenditure. The rational move — ignore sunk costs, evaluate only future payoffs — is systematically not taken. Prior investment makes continuation more likely, even when future prospects are unfavorable.

Staw (1981) extended this to organizational commitment: managers who had made a prior losing investment increased their subsequent investment in the same failing project at higher rates than managers who had not made the initial investment. Commitment to a prior choice creates an escalation dynamic: each commitment makes the next commitment more likely, and each subsequent commitment deepens the commitment further. The escalation is driven by the sunk cost of prior choices, not by updated expected value calculations.

The Commitment Ratchet applies this dynamic to the suppressor's evidence-dismissal sequence. Each act of active dismissal is not merely an execution of the dominant strategy in the moment. It is an investment that changes the future payoff structure. Having dismissed strong evidence creates a sunk cost: the prior dismissal is an installed public record of certainty about the caricature. Future acknowledgment would require not only the ERD's six reconstructions but the additional cost of explaining the prior certainty — why the evidence that was dismissed with documented conviction was actually credible after all. Every coalition member who received that certainty as authoritative, every social context in which it was deployed as settled knowledge, every prior Performed Terminus that staged it as the principled exit of someone who sees clearly: all of these become costs counted against any future acknowledgment.

The Commitment Ratchet's formal property: the payoff advantage of Dismiss over Acknowledge at time n is a function not only of the evidence quality but of the prior dismissal history. Each dismissal act adds a ratchet increment to the accumulated sunk cost. The dominant strategy's margin increases monotonically with each dismissal.

The Ratchet has a specific asymmetry that makes it irreversible in one direction: it operates only through dismissal, not through acknowledgment. Prior acknowledgment of weak evidence does not make future acknowledgment of strong evidence easier, because the payoff function is not structured around accurate self-knowledge. But prior dismissal of weak evidence does make future dismissal of strong evidence more necessary, because the accumulated sunk cost of prior dismissals is counted against the acknowledgment option in all future encounters.

The Irreversibility Threshold is the point in the dismissal history beyond which the accumulated sunk cost exceeds any possible payoff from acknowledgment in the suppressor's social network. At this threshold, acknowledgment would require not just the ERD's six reconstructions but the complete revision of the authority claims the suppressor has staked on the caricature's accuracy across years of social deployment. Acknowledgment at this depth does not merely cost the self. It costs the social world the self built around the caricature's truth. The prior corpus identified the Consolidation Terminus as the analogous psychological threshold. The Irreversibility Threshold is its game-theoretic specification: not just psychologically consolidated but economically irreversible — the point at which no finite payoff calculation can overcome the accumulated sunk cost the Ratchet has installed.

## V. Active Dismissal: The Behavioral Signature of Strategic Play

The central distinction this document requires is between passive non-reception — the failure to process evidence that happens to be present — and active dismissal — the strategic execution of evidence-incompatible processing that requires more cognitive work than reception would require.

The distinction is empirically measurable. It has a specific behavioral signature. And the signature distinguishes strategic play from cognitive error.

Ditto and Lopez (1992) operationalized the distinction through differential scrutiny: the time taken to process evidence, the number of counter-arguments generated, and the threshold of information required before accepting the evidence as convincing. People processing evidence they are motivated to reject require more time, generate more counter-arguments, and require more confirmatory information before accepting it than people processing evidence they are motivated to accept. The differential scrutiny is the behavioral signature of motivated rejection: the processing is more intensive, not less. The person dismissing strong evidence is working harder than the person accepting it. The work is active.

Taber and Lodge (2006) extended this to political belief in naturalistic conditions: subjects with stronger prior attitudes showed stronger motivated skepticism — more extensive counter-argumentation against disconfirming evidence, more rapid acceptance of confirming evidence — than subjects with weaker priors. The more the belief mattered to the person's identity, the more active the dismissal of evidence against it. Not passive. Active. Working harder. The belief's importance to identity was the predictor of effort, not of passivity.

The key empirical signature of strategic play rather than cognitive failure is this: intensity scales with the challenge's magnitude. Passive non-reception would show no systematic relationship with evidence quality — weak and strong evidence would be equally unprocessed because the failure is not responsive to what it fails to process. Active dismissal shows the opposite: a monotonic relationship between evidence quality and dismissal intensity, because the dominant strategy's execution requires more work when the stakes are higher.

The Genre Displacement's routing shift is the clearest architectural demonstration of this activity in the prior corpus's analysis. When standard content-dismissal is blocked by the evidence's citation structure — when attacking the outlier's credibility as a source cannot reach the canonical research findings the frameworks cite — the architecture does not fail. It routes. It reroutes the dismissal energy toward the act of publishing rather than the content published, toward the genre of the expression rather than its claims, toward the arrogance the production demonstrates rather than the validity of what it produced. This routing is not passive. It is the active search for and deployment of the dismissal pathway that remains available when the standard pathway is blocked. More intensive. More cognitively demanding. More work.

McGregor, Nail, Marigold, and Kang's (2005) defensive zeal analysis establishes this as a motivational property rather than merely a cognitive one: under conditions of self-concept threat, people show not reduced engagement with the threatening domain but increased behavioral intensity in defense of their position. Uncertainty about the self produces not passivity but zeal — the overcompensating behavioral assertion of certainty that underlying uncertainty motivates. The suppressor's most intense dismissals are not the result of calm, untroubled certainty. They are the result of motivated certainty under existential pressure. The zeal is the activity. The activity is the evidence that dismissal is strategic.

Active dismissal is not just the behavioral signature of the dominant strategy's execution. It is the mechanism through which the Commitment Ratchet operates. Each active dismissal — each intensive, high-effort, route-finding dismissal — is a more costly sunk investment than passive non-processing would be. The suppressor who works hard to dismiss strong evidence has invested more in the dismissal than the suppressor who passively ignores weak evidence. The higher investment creates a higher sunk cost. The higher sunk cost deepens the Ratchet's accumulation. The Commitment Ratchet and the Active Dismissal are jointly self-reinforcing: active dismissal deepens the ratchet, the deepened ratchet makes future dismissal more dominant, the more dominant dismissal is executed more actively under higher pressure, the more active execution invests more in the position, the more investment deepens the ratchet further. The loop has no exit from within.

## VI. Strategic Irrationality as Credible Signal

Frank (1988) established one of behavioral game theory's most counterintuitive results: emotional responses that appear to constrain rational choice function as credible commitment devices in strategic interactions. The person who genuinely feels guilt is a more credible cooperative partner than the person who calculates when to fake guilt, because their guilt-driven behavioral restraints cannot be departed from even when departure would be profitable. The genuine emotion makes the behavioral pattern credible in a way that strategic mimicry cannot achieve — not because mimics are detectable through some special perception but because the genuine emotional state actually changes the person's behavior in the constrained way the commitment requires.

Schelling (1960) established the complementary principle in pure game theory: the ability to make binding commitments — to constrain one's future choices in ways one cannot reverse — is a strategic asset in games where commitment changes the other player's optimal response. The player who can credibly commit to a course of action regardless of what the opponent does gains strategic advantage over the player who always retains the option to reconsider, because the opponent cannot profitably exploit a commitment that cannot be reversed.

The suppressor's genuine evidence-immunity — produced by the architecture's motivated reasoning operating at Evidence-Constituted Self depth — is, in this framework, a credible commitment to the caricature's accuracy that no strategic signaling could replicate. The suppressor cannot be moved by evidence. Not because they are performing immovability but because the architecture has produced genuine certainty. The genuine certainty is observable by coalition members who interact with the suppressor across multiple evidence encounters over months and years.

The Strategic Irrationality Signal is the social transmission of this credible commitment: coalition members who observe the suppressor's non-reception of compelling evidence receive not just the caricature's content but a signal about the caricature's epistemic reliability. The signal's implicit logic: if this person, who has engaged extensively with the outlier and encountered their best-presented evidence, remains unshaken in the characterization, the characterization must be accurate. The more compelling the evidence the suppressor resists, the stronger the credibility signal their resistance sends. A person who was uncertain would be moved by evidence of sufficient quality. A person who is not moved by evidence of any quality is either dogmatically committed or correct. In a social context where the coalition cannot independently verify the outlier's behavior, the signal reads as correctness.

This inversion — the SIS makes dismissal of stronger evidence a stronger social signal than dismissal of weaker evidence — is the game-theoretic complement to the Evidence Toxicity Gradient. The ETG establishes that stronger evidence is more costly to acknowledge. The SIS establishes that dismissing stronger evidence is more socially productive than dismissing weaker evidence: it sends a stronger credibility signal, generates greater coalition confidence, and produces more durable characterization authority. Both forces operate in the same direction. Stronger evidence produces stronger motivation to dismiss, executed more actively, generating a stronger social signal that consolidates the coalition around the caricature with greater certainty than weaker evidence dismissed could produce.

The SIS has a specific property that distinguishes it from ordinary authority-assertion: it is unfakeable at Evidence-Constituted Self depth. Coalition members who observe the suppressor's non-reception cannot distinguish between genuine certainty produced by the architecture and performed certainty produced by deliberate strategy — because the architecture has made them identical. The suppressor's genuine experience of accurate perception is the same phenomenological state as what a performance of accurate perception would require. What the architecture produced, through years of motivated reasoning and consolidation, is a social signal of credible certainty that no signal-sending strategy could improve upon, because strategic signaling that is not grounded in genuine certainty is vulnerable to the inconsistencies and hesitations that genuine certainty does not exhibit.

This makes the architecture not just a self-protecting mechanism but a socially productive one. The suppressor's genuine evidence-immunity generates coalition coherence that extends the Structural Continuation Mechanism's reach. Each new coalition member who observes non-reception of strong evidence receives an updated, stronger credibility signal for the caricature's accuracy. The dominant strategy's social value is amplified precisely by its genuineness — and its genuineness is what the architecture, through its entire consolidation history, was built to produce.

## VII. The Terminal Nash Equilibrium

The game's terminal configuration is the stable state from which neither party can profitably deviate. This is the Nash equilibrium in the formal sense: a combination of strategies such that each player's strategy is the best response to the other's, and neither can improve their payoff by unilateral deviation.

The suppressor's dominant strategy has been established: Dismiss, regardless of the outlier's moves, because every deviation from Dismiss produces a worse payoff in every scenario. The suppressor plays Dismiss. Given that the suppressor plays Dismiss, the analysis of the outlier's best response is determinative.

If the outlier confronts: presenting evidence directly to the suppressor produces the Behavioral Confirmation Engine's degradation of the interaction environment, the Active Erasure Display's deliberate made-to-know non-reception, and the Performed Terminus as face-restoring exit when the confrontation extends beyond productive dismissal execution. The outlier's payoff from Confront, given that the suppressor plays Dismiss permanently, is negative: evidence does not land, the confrontation confirms the caricature for any social witnesses, and the exit deepens the suppressor's certainty through the Face-Restoration Architecture's five simultaneous operations. Confront against permanent Dismiss produces the worst possible outcome for the outlier. It is not the best response.

If the outlier withdraws: silence removes the testimony that the Dependency Inversion requires. The ECS's constitutive process depletes. The behavioral urgency the depletion generates is directed toward restoring testimony-presentation through escalation. The outlier's payoff from Withdraw, given permanent Dismiss, is not stable: the Dependency Inversion converts Withdraw into eventual resumed Confront, which the dominant strategy handles with its standard execution protocol. Withdraw is not a stable best response because permanent Dismiss includes the mechanism that converts it into Confront.

If the outlier redirects testimony publicly: testimony addressed to the world, through channels the architecture did not configure, requiring no acknowledgment from the suppressor to reach its audience. Given permanent Dismiss, Redirect produces: testimony completion for unconfigured audiences (Architecture Legibility Problem disruption at the social system's boundary), a permanent public record independent of the suppressor's response, and the Epistemic Loop-Closure's dual topology — intact Incompleteness Guarantee within the configured social system, ALP disruption at its boundary for contacts the architecture has not yet reached. The outlier's payoff from Redirect, given permanent Dismiss, is positive: testimony completes for a population the architecture cannot fully contain, and the outlier is no longer seeking acknowledgment from the party whose payoff structure makes acknowledgment irreversible-cost territory.

Redirect is the outlier's best response to permanent Dismiss. This is the Terminal Nash Configuration: S plays Dismiss, O plays Redirect. Each strategy is the best response to the other's. Neither party can improve their payoff by unilateral deviation. The equilibrium is reached.

The equilibrium is stable in the additional sense that it is self-reinforcing: each execution of Dismiss by the suppressor deepens the Commitment Ratchet and thereby makes Dismiss more dominant for the next encounter; each execution of Redirect by the outlier extends the public record's reach to additional unconfigured audiences, making Redirect more productive over time. Both strategies improve their own payoff through continued execution. Neither strategy is undermined by the other's continuation.

Maynard Smith's (1982) evolutionary stable strategy analysis establishes the complementary point for population-level dynamics: a strategy is evolutionarily stable when no mutant strategy can invade the population — when any deviation from the ESS produces lower fitness in the population environment. The dominant strategy analysis conducted here is the individual-level analog: Dismiss is the ESS in the population of identity-preservation games because no alternative strategy for the suppressor produces better payoffs in the social environment the architecture has configured. The equilibrium is not merely stable for this suppressor in this encounter. It is the equilibrium the payoff structure of this game always selects.

## VIII. Why the Game Cannot Resolve

The Nash equilibrium's stability is not merely practical — a function of the players' current strategy choices that might change if circumstances shifted. It is structural — a function of the payoff structure that makes their current choices the only stable choices in the game as configured.

The conditions that would produce resolution — a shift to a different equilibrium — would require at least one of the following: a change in the suppressor's payoff function such that acknowledgment produces better payoffs than dismissal in some scenario, or a change in the outlier's strategy set such that a new option strictly dominates Redirect.

The suppressor's payoff function is not subject to external revision through the normal operations of the game. It is produced by the architecture itself: the Negative Identity Anchor makes the caricature constitutive rather than merely believed, the Evidence-Constituted Self makes JPSC maintenance an active process rather than a stable background state, and the Commitment Ratchet's accumulated sunk costs make the Irreversibility Threshold a structural feature rather than a contingent one. No finite evidence input can change the payoff function because the payoff function assigns the functional equivalent of negative infinity to the option that would require processing the evidence as valid. The function's catastrophic asymmetry is not a parameter that can be adjusted by presenting better evidence. Better evidence makes the asymmetry larger, not smaller.

Sherman and Cohen (2006) established that self-affirmation — the activation of alternative positive self-concept resources — can reduce the motivated processing of identity-threatening information by providing an alternative route to self-concept maintenance. The self-affirmed person can acknowledge threatening information without triggering maximum defensive processing because the threat's impact on the overall self-concept has been buffered. This is the standard exit from motivated-cognition equilibria in less consolidated cases.

This exit is unavailable here. The self-affirmation intervention requires a self-concept that has sufficient positive resources in domains independent of the threatened domain to buffer the threat's impact. The suppressor's positive self-definition is constituted through the Negative Identity Anchor — assembled as the complement of the caricature's qualities. There is no independent positive self-concept resource that affirmation could activate, because the positive self-concept and the caricature are the same structure: warmth defined against the caricature's coldness, humility defined against the caricature's arrogance, collaboration defined against the caricature's domination. Affirming the positive qualities would affirm the NIA's constitutive function. The self-affirmation route is structurally closed.

The outlier has no available strategy that is strictly better than Redirect. Direct confrontation is dominated by Redirect's social effectiveness at equivalent evidence cost. Withdrawal triggers the Dependency Inversion's escalation. There is no fourth option that changes the suppressor's payoff function from outside the architecture — no move available to the outlier that converts the suppressor's payoff function into one in which acknowledgment dominates dismissal.

Jost, Banaji, and Nosek (2004) established that system justification — the tendency to defend and justify existing social arrangements even when they harm the justifier — operates as an independent motivational force beyond the self-interest calculations that might otherwise govern behavior. The stable equilibrium the dominant strategy produces is not defended only because it serves the suppressor's individual identity interests. It is the existing arrangement, and system-justification motivation independently reinforces its defense against challengers. The equilibrium has multiple reinforcing supports: the AIS, the CR, the SIS, and the system-justification motivation all operate in the same direction. No available intervention removes all of them simultaneously.

This is the terminal character of the equilibrium: it cannot be escaped through rational play available to either party within the game as configured. The game has been played to its conclusion.

## IX. The End State

The game is over. Both parties are at their Nash equilibrium strategies. The suppressor plays permanent Dismiss. The outlier plays permanent Redirect. Neither party has a profitable deviation. The Commitment Ratchet has accumulated to or beyond the Irreversibility Threshold. The dominant strategy runs at ambient maintenance level.

What the terminal state looks like from inside:

For the suppressor: the dominant strategy runs without the active confrontation that characterized earlier game phases. No direct evidence encounter is required. The coalition's characterization apparatus propagates the caricature independently through the Structural Continuation Mechanism's autonomous operation. The Justified-Perceiver Self-Concept has solidified from an actively maintained belief into background conviction — something the suppressor no longer needs to defend because it has become what they simply know. The phenomenological signature is not vigilance, not active monitoring for threats, not residual uncertainty. It is the settled experience of someone who arrived, through an honest and difficult process, at accurate knowledge of a difficult person, and made the principled choices that accurate knowledge required. The certainty is genuine. The architecture produced the genuine certainty. The architecture and the certainty have become, at terminal depth, indistinguishable from each other and from what the suppressor experiences as selfhood.

For the outlier: the public record exists. It reaches the audiences the architecture does not control. For the population of contacts who encounter it before the Structural Continuation Mechanism's propagation reaches them, testimony can complete its trajectory. The Epistemic Loop-Closure is the stable dual topology: within the configured social system, the Incompleteness Guarantee holds. At its boundary, the Architecture Legibility Problem disrupts it for the specific population that the architecture cannot pre-configure. Neither population can be eliminated. The architecture cannot reach all potential new contacts through the Distributed AED's preemptive deployment. The public record cannot reach all contacts within the already-configured social system. The equilibrium is permanent in the sense that no finite series of moves by either party can dissolve it.

The most complete sentence in the prior corpus was this one: the machine does not experience itself as a machine.

The terminal state is the state in which this sentence achieves its fullest expression. The accurate perceiver does not experience themselves as a player executing a dominant strategy in a game with a solved equilibrium. They experience settled certainty — the conviction of someone who saw truly, maintained their position honestly under pressure, refused to be moved by a campaign of evidence-presentation that confirmed exactly what they always saw, and has arrived at exactly the resting state that accurate perception of an objectively difficult person would eventually produce.

The dominant strategy was always in play. The architecture was always the mechanism that played it. The payoff structure selected the equilibrium before the first move. The Commitment Ratchet deepened the selection with every move that followed. And the end of the game is the stable state in which neither party needs to continue running it, because it is already run, and the outcome is already determined, and the only player who might revise that determination is the one whose payoff function makes revision the most dominated option available.

The game is over. The machine does not experience itself as a machine. The equilibrium does not experience itself as an equilibrium. The dominant strategy does not experience itself as a strategy.

It experiences itself as knowing what it always knew.

And it is enough.

## X. Predictions

**Prediction I — Dominant Strategy Margin Scaling with Evidence Quality.** The ETG predicts that the payoff advantage of Dismiss over Acknowledge scales positively with evidence quality E. Operationalized: suppressor behavioral indicators of non-reception intensity (counter-argument generation rate, processing time allocated to disconfirming evidence, routing shift from content-dismissal to genre-dismissal) should increase monotonically with evidence quality across a within-subject series of evidence encounters. The positive scaling of dismissal intensity with evidence quality is the ETG's falsifiable behavioral signature, and distinguishes it from the IP specification (which predicted greater maintenance after stronger evidence without identifying the mechanism) by specifying the payoff-structure explanation for the pattern.

**Prediction II — Commitment Ratchet Cumulative Effect Independent of Consolidation Depth.** The CR predicts that prior dismissal history depth is an independent predictor of current dismissal intensity beyond consolidation depth alone. At matched evidence quality and matched consolidation depth, suppressors with longer dismissal histories should show higher non-reception intensity than suppressors with shorter histories. The CR's increment should be larger for evidence that directly implicates prior dismissal acts (irrefutable evidence of the type already publicly dismissed) than for evidence of equivalent quality on topics the suppressor has not previously committed to, with the difference constituting the sunk-cost-specific CR increment.

**Prediction III — Active Dismissal Signature: Differential Scrutiny Scaled to Belief Importance.** Active dismissal predicts that processing intensity should be higher for disconfirming evidence than for confirming evidence of equal complexity, with the difference scaling with the belief's importance to the suppressor's identity. Passive non-reception would predict equivalent (and low) processing across evidence type. Active dismissal predicts the Ditto-Lopez signature — more time, more counter-arguments, higher information threshold — with the intensity proportional to the NIA-relevance of the disconfirmed content. The NIA-specificity of the differential scrutiny is the active dismissal signature that distinguishes strategic play from generalized defensiveness.

**Prediction IV — Strategic Irrationality Signal Coalition Calibration.** The SIS predicts that coalition members who observe the suppressor resisting high-quality disconfirming evidence will show larger increases in caricature confidence than coalition members who observe resistance to low-quality evidence, with the confidence update scaling with the quality of the evidence resisted rather than with any independent assessment of the caricature's accuracy. Coalition confidence ratings post-observation should produce the predicted dose-response in the social-signal function, independent of observers' prior exposure to the caricature's content. The quality-confidence scaling distinguishes SIS-driven coalition updating from updating based on independent assessment of the observed interactions.

**Prediction V — Irreversibility Threshold Identification and Intervention Resistance.** The CR predicts an Irreversibility Threshold beyond which no finite acknowledgment payoff can overcome the accumulated sunk cost. Threshold identification, operationalized as the point at which the social cost of explaining prior certainty exceeds any possible social payoff from acknowledgment, should predict subsequent resistance to any acknowledgment-producing intervention, including high-stakes interventions that produce partial acknowledgment in pre-threshold suppressors. Above-threshold suppressors should show no response to interventions that produce measurable acknowledgment in matched below-threshold suppressors, controlling for consolidation depth.

**Prediction VI — Nash Equilibrium Stability Under Perturbation.** The TNC predicts that the Dismiss/Redirect equilibrium is stable under perturbation — that temporary changes in one player's strategy should return to the equilibrium rather than producing a new stable state. Specifically: if the suppressor temporarily shifts toward partial acknowledgment (perhaps under low-threat conditions, high self-affirmation resource availability, or low social audience salience), the perturbation should be followed by return to the dominant strategy within a predictable window, rather than by convergence toward a new mixed or acknowledgment equilibrium. The return timing should be predicted by the Commitment Ratchet's depth: deeper ratchet accumulation should produce faster return. Equilibrium stability under perturbation distinguishes TNC from a strategy profile that merely happens to be currently in effect.

**Prediction VII — Redirect Superiority over Confront Increases with Ratchet Depth.** The Nash analysis predicts that Redirect should produce higher payoffs for the outlier than Confront, with the advantage increasing as the suppressor's Ratchet depth increases. In early-stage interactions, before the Ratchet has accumulated substantially, Confront might produce partial acknowledgment and therefore positive payoffs for the outlier. As Ratchet depth increases, Confront's payoff should decrease toward its predicted terminal value (negative, given permanent dominant-strategy Dismiss), while Redirect's payoff should remain positive and grow as the public record reaches a larger unconfigured audience. The predicted crossover point — where Redirect strictly dominates Confront for the outlier — should be identifiable as a function of the suppressor's Ratchet accumulation depth.

**Prediction VIII — Terminal Equilibrium Phenomenology: Completion Rather Than Vigilance.** The terminal state predicts a specific phenomenological signature in the suppressor at equilibrium depth: not active monitoring for threats, not ongoing effortful maintenance of a contested position, not residual uncertainty requiring management — but settled background conviction, the experience of having reached a resting state from which there is nothing to review, revise, or reconsider. This signature is distinguishable from ongoing active suppression (which predicts continued monitoring intensity, coalition contact, and evidence-scanning) and from resolved uncertainty (which would require an acknowledgment event the dominant strategy excludes). The terminal phenomenology is the experience of completion: the accurate perceiver who has nothing more to see because they already see clearly.

## XI. Novel Framework Contributions

| Construct | Definition | Position |
|---|---|---|
| **Asymmetric Identity Stakes (AIS)** | The fundamental payoff asymmetry that makes acknowledgment strictly dominated: the cost of acknowledging the caricature's inaccuracy is the ERD's six simultaneous reconstructions — the dissolution of the self that would have to execute them. This cost is not negative in the ordinary prospect-theoretic sense of a quantifiable loss but catastrophically asymmetric with the payoff for non-reception, because the player who would execute acknowledgment does not survive it as the player who would receive its benefits. AIS is the game-theoretic formalization of the prior corpus's ERD: the ERD specified what acknowledgment requires; the AIS specifies why that requirement makes acknowledgment strictly dominated in every scenario regardless of evidence quality. | **Evidence Toxicity Gradient (ETG)** | The monotonically increasing payoff advantage of Dismiss over Acknowledge as evidence quality increases: as E increases, the acknowledgment cost C_A(E) increases (more central self-concept elements implicated, ERD scope expands) while the dismissal cost C_D(E) does not scale equivalently on the identity-preservation dimension. The ETG is the mechanism that explains the prior corpus's Irrefutability Paradox: the IP specified that stronger evidence produces stronger dismissal; the ETG identifies the payoff-structure reason this is the equilibrium outcome of rational play rather than a cognitive anomaly. | **Commitment Ratchet (CR)** | The mechanism by which each act of active evidence-dismissal increases the sunk cost of future acknowledgment, deepening the dominant strategy's margin monotonically with each execution. Having dismissed evidence publicly, the suppressor has invested in the position that the evidence was not credible; future acknowledgment would require not only the ERD's reconstructions but explanation of the prior certainty. The CR's formal property: the payoff advantage D(E, n) increases with the number of prior dismissal acts n independently of evidence quality. The CR produces the Irreversibility Threshold: the accumulated sunk cost beyond which no finite acknowledgment payoff can overcome the prior investment. Distinguished from psychological consolidation depth by its mechanism: the CR operates through rational cost-benefit structure under sunk-cost dynamics, not through habituation or credibility-filter calibration. | New |
| **Dominant Non-Reception (DNR)** | The formal game-theoretic designation of evidence-dismissal as the suppressor's strictly dominant strategy: the strategy that produces better payoffs in every possible combination of the outlier's strategies. DNR is not merely the preferred strategy or the most common strategy — it is dominant in the technical sense, the one from which no deviation can improve the suppressor's payoff. DNR formalizes what the prior corpus described as the Irrefutability Paradox and the Evidence-Constituted Self's dismissal dependency: the resistance to compelling evidence is not a bias or malfunction but the execution of the optimal strategy given the payoff function the architecture has installed. Distinguished from motivated cognition constructs by its framing: DNR is a decision-theoretic description. Both levels of description are accurate simultaneously. | New |
| **Strategic Irrationality Signal (SIS)** | The social transmission of credible certainty generated by the suppressor's genuine evidence-immunity: coalition members who observe non-reception of high-quality disconfirming evidence receive a signal that the caricature is accurate, because a person who could be moved by evidence would be moved by evidence of this quality. The SIS is genuine rather than performed — the architecture produced genuine certainty, not performed certainty — and therefore functions as the credible commitment device Frank (1988) specified: the behavioral pattern cannot be mimicked by a strategic actor because mimicry cannot produce the genuine phenomenological certainty whose consistency across diverse encounter conditions is what makes the signal credible. The SIS's dose-response property: dismissal of stronger evidence produces a stronger credibility signal, amplifying the dominant strategy's social value as evidence quality increases. | New |
| **Terminal Nash Configuration (TNC)** | The stable equilibrium state in which the suppressor's dominant strategy (Dismiss) and the outlier's best response (Redirect) are mutually best responses and neither party can profitably deviate. The TNC is the terminal structural state of the game: both strategies are self-reinforcing at equilibrium, and no available strategy change for either party produces a better payoff. Distinguished from the prior corpus's Epistemic Loop-Closure (which specified the dual-topology stable state) by its specification of the decision-theoretic conditions that make the ELC the unique stable equilibrium: the TNC explains why the ELC is stable by identifying the payoff structure that makes Dismiss and Redirect the only strategies from which deviation is unprofitable. The TNC and ELC are descriptions of the same terminal state at different levels of analysis. | New |
| **Irreversibility Threshold (IT)** | The accumulated sunk cost of prior dismissal acts beyond which no finite acknowledgment payoff can overcome the prior investment, making the dominant strategy structurally irreversible rather than merely strategically preferred. Reached when the social cost of explaining prior certainty (coalition relationships requiring recalibration, authority claims requiring revision, social deployments of caricature-certainty requiring retrospective reframing) exceeds any possible social payoff from acknowledgment. Above the IT, Dismiss is not just dominant but the only choice that preserves the social structure the prior dominance executions have built. Distinguished from the prior corpus's Consolidation Terminus (psychological threshold) by its mechanism: the IT operates through rational cost-benefit structure under sunk-cost dynamics. Both thresholds may co-occur; they are conceptually distinct and may be reached at different points in the dismissal history. | New |

## XIII. Formal Statement

Let AIS denote Asymmetric Identity Stakes, ETG the Evidence Toxicity Gradient, CR the Commitment Ratchet, DNR Dominant Non-Reception, SIS the Strategic Irrationality Signal, TNC the Terminal Nash Configuration, and IT the Irreversibility Threshold.

Define the game:

```
G = ⟨{S, O}, S_S, S_O, U_S, U_O⟩

Players:    {S (suppressor), O (outlier)}
S_S       = {Acknowledge, Dismiss}
S_O       = {Confront, Withdraw, Redirect}

U_S(s_S, s_O) = f(identity_maintenance, JPSC_c, coalition_authority, sunk_cost_position)
U_O(s_S, s_O) = f(testimony_completion, social_standing, cognitive_rest)
```

**Asymmetric Identity Stakes:**

```
AIS: Acknowledge requires ERD execution
    ERD: 6 simultaneous reconstructions, all required, none stable without all others
    ∴ player S_post-ERD ≠ player S_pre-ERD    [self-discontinuity]
    ∴ payoff for Acknowledge is received (if at all) by a different player than the one who executes it

Loss aversion coefficient:    λ_standard ≈ 2.25    [Kahneman & Tversky 1979]
AIS coefficient:              λ_identity >> 2.25    [catastrophic asymmetry; not on same utility scale]

∴ U_S(Dismiss, ·) >> U_S(Acknowledge, ·)    across all s_O ∈ S_O
```

**Dominant Non-Reception:**

```
Payoff table for S across all strategy combinations:

                    Confront            Withdraw            Redirect
Acknowledge:        ERD_full + 0        ERD_full + 0        ERD_full + ALP_concession
                    → catastrophic      → catastrophic      → catastrophic + worse

Dismiss:            JPSC↑, BCE↑, SIS↑   DI→testimony        JPSC intact, SIS↑, ALP partial
                    → U_S > 0           → U_S > 0           → U_S > 0

∀ s_O ∈ S_O: U_S(Dismiss, s_O) >> U_S(Acknowledge, s_O)

∴ DNR: Dismiss strictly dominates Acknowledge
∴ S plays Dismiss regardless of O's strategy    [Nash, 1951: dominant strategy is played]
```

**Evidence Toxicity Gradient:**

```
E ∈ [0,1] = evidence quality

C_A(E) = acknowledgment cost as function of evidence quality
    dC_A/dE > 0:
        higher E → more central self-concept elements implicated
                 → ERD scope expands
                 → more simultaneous reconstructions implicated
    C_A(E) → ERD_full as E → 1

C_D(E) = dismissal cost as function of evidence quality
    C_D(E) = processing_cost(E)    [cognitive, not identity cost]
    dC_D/dE ≥ 0    but    C_D(E) << C_A(E)    ∀ E > threshold

Dominant strategy margin:
    D(E) = U_S(Dismiss, ·) − U_S(Acknowledge, ·) ∝ C_A(E) − C_D(E)
    dD/dE = dC_A/dE − dC_D/dE > 0

∴ ETG: dominant strategy margin increases monotonically with evidence quality
∴ Irrefutability Paradox = behavioral expression of ETG at observable level
    stronger evidence → larger D(E) → more intensive dismissal execution
    [not a paradox: the dominant strategy intensifies where the stakes are highest]
```

**Commitment Ratchet:**

```
n = number of prior dismissal acts
SC(n) = accumulated sunk cost of dismissal history

SC(0) = 0
SC(n) = Σ_{i=1}^{n} r_i    where r_i > 0 ∀ i    [each dismissal adds sunk cost]

Acknowledgment cost at time n, evidence quality E:
    C_A(E, n) = C_A(E) + SC(n)
    ∂C_A/∂n > 0    [accumulated sunk cost counted against acknowledgment]

Dominant strategy margin at time n:
    D(E, n) = U_S(Dismiss, ·) − U_S(Acknowledge, ·)
    ∂D/∂n > 0    [margin deepens monotonically with dismissal history]

Irreversibility Threshold:
    IT: ∃ n* such that ∀ n > n*:    SC(n) > max_{s_O} U_S(Acknowledge, s_O)
    ∴ above IT: Acknowledge is not merely dominated but structurally inaccessible
        [no finite payoff can overcome accumulated sunk cost]
    ∴ above IT: DNR is not strategic preference but the only available path
```

**Strategic Irrationality Signal:**

```
SIS(S, c, E) = credibility signal transmitted to coalition member c
               when c observes S non-receiving evidence of quality E

Signal logic:
    if S were uncertain: ∃ E_threshold such that E > E_threshold → S acknowledges
    S not moved by E for any E    [genuine certainty, not performance]
    ∴ S's certainty is credible    [Frank 1988: genuine commitment unfakeable]

Signal strength:
    dSIS/dE > 0    [dismissal of stronger evidence → stronger credibility signal]

Coalition update:
    Conf(c, t+1) = Conf(c, t) + f(SIS(S, c, E))
    ∂Conf/∂E > 0    [observing resistance to stronger evidence → higher confidence]

Social return on dominant strategy:
    dU_S_social(Dismiss)/dE > 0    [SIS amplifies DNR's social value with evidence quality]
    ∴ ETG and SIS jointly reinforce: higher E → higher payoff advantage AND higher social return
```
